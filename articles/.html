The Hidden Gaps in Your Property Insurance That Could Cost You Everything

Last month, I reviewed a policy for a 28-unit garden-style complex in Phoenix. The owner had been with the same carrier for eight years, premium had stayed relatively stable, and he thought he was well-protected. Then we ran the numbers on his replacement cost coverage: $150 per square foot on a building that would cost $230 per square foot to rebuild today. He was underinsured by nearly $800,000 on a $2.4 million property. His agent had been keeping his limits artificially low to maintain "competitive" premiums, but competitive premiums don't rebuild your property after a total loss.

This scenario plays out daily across the country. Construction costs have exploded, coverage gaps have widened, and many property owners are discovering their insurance shortfalls only when they need to file a claim. Meanwhile, carriers are tightening underwriting standards, pulling back from certain coverages, and in markets like California, some are exiting apartment coverage entirely.

The insurance landscape for multifamily properties has fundamentally shifted in the past five years. What worked before won't protect you now. Here's what you need to know to properly insure your properties in today's market.

The Underinsurance Crisis: Why Your Building Limits Are Probably Wrong

The number one coverage gap I see nationwide is inadequate building limits. Many owners are carrying coverage based on pre-2020 construction costs, which is a recipe for disaster.

Current realistic replacement costs vary dramatically by region. In California, you're looking at $300-400 per square foot for quality multifamily construction. Midwest markets like Ohio or Michigan typically run $200-250 per square foot, while Arizona falls somewhere in between. These aren't inflated estimates—they're what it actually costs to rebuild with current labor rates, material costs, and building codes.

Your Total Insured Value (TIV) includes your building limit plus business personal property plus business income limits. This TIV is what carriers use to calculate your property premium—typically 15 to 50+ cents per $100 of TIV, depending on your property's risk profile and location. Many agents keep building limits artificially low to reduce TIV and keep premiums down, but this creates a false economy. You're saving money on premiums while exposing yourself to potentially catastrophic underinsurance.

Here's what underinsurance actually costs: If you have a coinsurance clause (typically 80% or 90%) and you're underinsured, you become a co-insurer for every claim. A $50,000 roof claim on an underinsured property might only net you $35,000 after the coinsurance penalty. On a total loss, you could be looking at hundreds of thousands in uncovered costs.

The solution is straightforward but requires discipline: Get a current replacement cost valuation from a qualified appraiser, not an online calculator. Budget for the premium increase. Your building limit should reflect today's construction reality, not what you wish it would cost to rebuild.

Coverage Gaps That Can Sink Your Investment

Beyond underinsurance, several coverage gaps have become particularly problematic as carriers tighten their underwriting.

Building ordinance and law coverage is critical for older properties but often missing or inadequate. When you rebuild after a covered loss, you must comply with current building codes, not the codes from when your property was originally constructed. This can add 25-50% to reconstruction costs, and basic property policies typically provide minimal ordinance coverage.

Loss of rents coverage—now often called habitability coverage—is being restricted by carriers through exclusions or sublimits. This coverage pays your lost rental income when units become uninhabitable due to a covered loss. Carriers are increasingly concerned about habitability claims dragging on for months, especially when properties have deferred maintenance issues that complicate repairs.

Assault and battery exclusions represent another area where carriers are pulling back significantly. These incidents in multifamily properties can generate substantial liability claims, and many carriers now exclude them entirely or provide very limited coverage.

Wrongful eviction coverage deserves special attention. Eviction laws have become increasingly tenant-favorable, and the legal costs of defending an eviction—even when you win—can be substantial. Many standard policies exclude this coverage entirely.

Pollution exclusions have also expanded. Carriers routinely exclude claims related to mold, legionella, lead paint, and other environmental issues. For apartment owners, mold claims can be particularly expensive, often running $20,000-50,000 per unit for proper remediation.

The California Challenge: Why the Golden State Requires Special Attention

California presents unique challenges that require specific insurance strategies. Courts consistently favor tenants, plaintiff attorneys are aggressive, and reconstruction costs are among the highest in the nation.

The legal environment in California is particularly challenging for property owners. Plaintiff attorneys routinely raise habitability issues in any claim, turning simple slip-and-fall incidents into complex cases about deferred maintenance, garbage in common areas, or slow response to repair requests. What starts as a minor liability claim can quickly escalate when opposing counsel argues that poor property conditions contributed to the incident.

Many national carriers have pulled back from California apartment coverage or dramatically tightened their underwriting standards. This has created capacity issues and pushed many properties into surplus lines markets or California's FAIR Plan.

The FAIR Plan, California's insurer of last resort, provides basic fire coverage but lacks comprehensive protection. Properties using FAIR Plan typically need a Difference in Conditions (DIC) policy to provide broader coverage for theft, liability, and other perils. This two-policy approach can work but requires careful coordination to avoid gaps.

For California properties, umbrella coverage isn't optional—it's essential. General liability rates for apartments typically run $100-200 per door annually, but in California's litigious environment, even minor incidents can generate claims exceeding your primary liability limits. Properties with pools, elevators, or prior claims should expect higher rates.

What Most Property Owners Get Wrong About Commercial Insurance

The biggest mistake I see is treating insurance like a commodity purchase. Property owners will spend weeks researching which water heater to install but make insurance decisions based solely on premium comparisons.

Insurance coverage varies dramatically between carriers and policies. A $500 annual premium difference between two quotes often reflects meaningful coverage differences, not just pricing variations. The cheaper policy might exclude wrongful eviction, provide minimal ordinance coverage, or have a higher deductible that wasn't clearly explained.

Another common error is neglecting to update coverage as properties are improved. You renovate units, upgrade HVAC systems, or add amenities, but your insurance limits remain static. Your coverage should evolve with your property.

Many owners also misunderstand how general liability coverage works for multifamily properties. They assume their property insurance covers everything, but liability claims—slip and falls, dog bites, injuries in common areas—are covered under general liability, not property coverage. These are separate coverages with separate limits, and you need adequate limits for both.

Finally, too many property owners wait until renewal to review their coverage. Insurance markets change quickly, new exclusions appear, and coverage forms are updated. An annual insurance review with a knowledgeable agent should be standard practice, not something you do only when premiums increase dramatically.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much general liability coverage do I need for my apartment building?

Start with $1 million per occurrence and $2 million aggregate as a baseline, but consider higher limits based on your property size and location. Budget $100-200 per door annually for GL coverage, with higher costs for properties with pools, elevators, or prior claims. In litigious markets like California, umbrella coverage above your primary limits is essential.

Should I choose a higher deductible to lower my premium?

Higher deductibles can meaningfully reduce premiums, but ensure you can comfortably handle the deductible amount for multiple claims in a single year. A $10,000 deductible might save you $2,000 annually in premium, but if you have three claims in one year, you're paying $30,000 out of pocket. Consider your cash flow and claims history when making this decision.

What's the difference between replacement cost and actual cash value coverage?

Replacement cost pays to rebuild or repair with new materials at current costs. Actual cash value deducts depreciation, leaving you with less money to complete repairs. For income-producing properties, replacement cost coverage is worth the additional premium—you need your building restored to rentable condition, not partially compensated for a depreciated asset.

Do I need business income coverage if my property is fully occupied?

Absolutely. Business income coverage pays lost rents when your property becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss. Even if you're fully occupied today, a fire or other covered loss could leave units unrentable for months during repairs. This coverage should typically equal 12-18 months of gross rental income.

How do I know if I'm working with a qualified apartment insurance agent?

Look for agents who specialize in multifamily properties and can discuss specific coverage issues like habitability exclusions, building ordinance coverage, and coinsurance clauses. They should understand current construction costs in your market and be able to explain why coverage limits are set at specific amounts. If an agent focuses primarily on premium comparisons without discussing coverage details, find someone else.

Moving Forward: Building a Comprehensive Insurance Strategy

The multifamily insurance market will likely remain challenging for the foreseeable future. Carriers are maintaining strict underwriting standards, coverage gaps are widening, and premiums continue to increase in many markets. However, property owners who understand these dynamics and work with qualified agents can still obtain comprehensive protection.

Focus on coverage adequacy first, premium optimization second. Ensure your building limits reflect current replacement costs, maintain adequate liability limits, and address common coverage gaps before they become expensive problems. The cost of proper insurance coverage is a small fraction of your property's value and rental income—it's not the place to cut corners.

Work with agents and carriers who understand multifamily properties. Specialists like Honeycomb, Distinguished Programs, and others focus specifically on apartment buildings and understand the unique risks you face. Your insurance program should be designed by people who understand your business, not generic commercial agents treating your property like any other commercial risk.
